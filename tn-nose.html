Turismo Nacional Front Nose Aerodynamic Redesign

Redesign of a Turismo Nacional front nose and fender assembly aimed at improving aerodynamic performance, developed from trackside observations and CAD modeling to prototype support and on-track validation.

Detroit Racing | 2025

Overview

This project originated during race weekend engineering support, where an opportunity was identified to improve the aerodynamic performance of the car through a redesign of the front nose and fender geometry. The work evolved from initial concept development to CAD redesign, prototype support, manufacturing, and experimental on-track evaluation.

Role and Contributions

My contribution combined trackside engineering involvement with hands-on design work. After identifying the potential for improvement, I participated in the redesign of the front-end surfaces in SolidWorks, considering both aerodynamic intent and practical manufacturing constraints. I also supported the development of prototype insert models through 3D printing and followed the project through implementation and testing.

  • Detected an aerodynamic improvement opportunity while supporting the team at race events.
  • Redesigned the front nose and fender surfaces in SolidWorks.
  • Balanced aerodynamic objectives with manufacturing and real-world track-use constraints.
  • Produced 3D-printed insert models to support prototype development.
  • Participated in the implementation of the modified front-end assembly on the race car.
  • Contributed to the experimental evaluation of the redesign through data acquisition analysis.

Design Process

One of the most valuable aspects of this project was following the full engineering path from observation to validation. The redesign was not limited to proposing a new shape: it required building a feasible geometry, adapting it to the vehicle, supporting the manufacturing process, and ensuring that the final solution could be tested under actual competition conditions.

Experimental Validation

After implementation, the modified front-end configuration was evaluated experimentally using track data acquisition. The results showed a slight reduction in drag, providing a measurable indication that the redesign moved in the intended aerodynamic direction. Even though the gain was moderate, the project was especially valuable because it linked design decisions with real on-track performance data.

Project Value

This experience strengthened my understanding of motorsport engineering as a process that connects trackside observation, CAD development, prototyping, and validation. It was also an important opportunity to work in a junior engineering role within a competitive racing environment, contributing to a real vehicle development task with measurable results.
